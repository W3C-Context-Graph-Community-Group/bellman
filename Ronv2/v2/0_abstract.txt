The Dark Fraction Theorem:
A Geometric Bound on Unverifiable Configurations
at System Boundaries
Ron Itelman∗ Jacek Kowalski†
April 2026
Working paper

Abstract
We present a protocol that decomposes each variable at a system boundary into three
binary facets—Meaning, Structure, and Context—and show that this decomposition induces
a Hamming cube {0, 1}³ᵐ as the natural configuration space for m variables. Verification
of r facets covers a Hamming ball whose volume is computable exactly. The ratio of ball to
cube—the verified fraction—goes to zero as m grows. The complement—the dark fraction—
measures the portion of the configuration space that no within-system diagnostic can reach.
This quantity is new: no prior framework provides a computable metric for unverifiable
boundary configurations. The mathematics is standard. The contribution is the mapping
from protocol to geometry and the predictions it enables.

1 The Protocol
When data crosses a boundary between two systems, each variable carries a value and an inter-
pretation. The protocol requires the interpretation to be registered through three facets, each
resolved to a URL:

Facet          What is registered
Meaning        What the value refers to (e.g., trade execution date vs.
               settlement date)
Structure      How the value is encoded (e.g., MM/DD/YYYY vs.
               DD/MM/YYYY)
Context        Conditions under which Meaning and Structure hold
               (e.g., US market / NYSE / Eastern time)

A fourth facet, Data, is the raw value itself—it crosses the boundary directly and requires no
registration. The three registered facets produce the binary coordinates used in the proof.

At every boundary, the protocol compares each shared variable’s URLs between sender and
receiver. For each facet f ∈ {M, S, C} of variable vᵢ, the comparison produces one bit:

ωᶠᵢ = ( 0 if the URLs match (aligned)
        1 if the URLs do not match (misaligned) )

Figure 2: Dark uncertainty at a boundary between two coherence units. The string
"05/04/2026" is identical in both, but Coherence Unit A reads it as May 4th (MM/DD/YYYY)

facet configuration. The misalignment is invisible to any system that checks only the raw value.

This output is not a modeling simplification applied to a richer space. It is the instrument’s
native resolution. URLs either match or they do not. There is no partial match, no degree of
similarity, no continuous distance. The binary encoding is inherent.

2 The Configuration Space
Let a boundary involve m shared variables. Each variable produces three bits (one per facet).
The full configuration vector is:

ω = (ωᴹ₁ , ωˢ₁ , ωᶜ₁ , ωᴹ₂ , ωˢ₂ , ωᶜ₂ , … , ωᴹₘ , ωˢₘ, ωᶜₘ) ∈ {0, 1}³ᵐ

Let n = 3m.

Definition 1 (Coherence unit). A single variable vᵢ together with its three facet comparisons
(ωᴹᵢ , ωˢᵢ , ωᶜᵢ ) ∈ {0, 1}³ is a coherence unit. It is the atomic element of boundary measurement:
the smallest object whose alignment state is independently verifiable. A boundary with m shared
variables comprises m coherence units, and the full configuration space is the product of their
individual cubes: {0, 1}³ × ⋯ × {0, 1}³ = {0, 1}³ᵐ.

The configuration space is the Hamming cube:

Ω = {0, 1}ⁿ, |Ω| = 2ⁿ

The aligned configuration ω∗ = (0, 0, …, 0) is the state where every URL matches—no mis-
alignment on any facet of any variable.

Definition 2 (Hamming distance). For ω, ω′ ∈ {0, 1}ⁿ:

dₕ(ω, ω′) = Σⱼ₌₁ⁿ |ωⱼ − ω′ⱼ|

This counts the number of facets on which two configurations disagree. It requires only the
comparison “same or different”—exactly the comparison the protocol performs. The metric is
produced by the instrument, not imposed on the problem.

Definition 3 (Hamming ball). The Hamming ball of radius r centered at ω∗:

Bᵣ(ω∗) = {ω ∈ Ω : dₕ(ω, ω∗) ≤ r}

with volume:

|Bᵣ| = Σₖ₌₀ʳ (n choose k)

3 The Dark Fraction Theorem
Theorem 4 (Dark Fraction Bound). Let Ω = {0, 1}ⁿ with n = 3m be the configuration space of
a boundary with m shared variables. Let r facets be verified through cross-boundary measurement.
The verified fraction is:

ϕ(n, r) = 1/2ⁿ ⋅ Σₖ₌₀ʳ (n choose k)

The dark fraction—the fraction of configurations that no within-boundary diagnostic can reach—
is:

δ(n, r) = 1 − ϕ(n, r)

Proof. Verification of a facet determines its value: 0 or 1. A configuration ω is covered by r
verifications if it differs from ω∗ in at most r positions—that is, if dₕ(ω, ω∗) ≤ r. The set of such
configurations is Bᵣ(ω∗). Its cardinality is Σₖ₌₀ʳ (n choose k) (choosing which k of n positions differ, for
each k ≤ r). The cube has 2ⁿ elements. The ratio follows.

Corollary 5 (Concentration). For any fixed verification budget r:

limₙ→∞ ϕ(n, r) = 0

Proof. |Bᵣ| is a polynomial in n of degree r. |Ω| = 2ⁿ is exponential. Polynomial divided by
exponential vanishes.

Corollary 6 (Marginal return). The reduction in dark fraction from verifying the (r + 1)-th
facet is:

ϕ(n, r + 1) − ϕ(n, r) = 1/2ⁿ ⋅ (n choose r+1)

Proof. The ball of radius r + 1 contains exactly (n choose r+1) configurations not in the ball of radius
r—those at Hamming distance exactly r + 1 from ω∗.

3.2 Asymptotic form
For r = αn with α < 1/2:

δ ≈ 1 − 2^{n(H₂(α) − 1)}

where H₂(α) = −α log₂ α − (1−α) log₂(1−α) is the binary entropy. Since H₂(α) < 1 for α < 1/2,
the dark fraction approaches 1 exponentially in n.

4 Predictions
The mapping from four-facet protocol to Hamming geometry produces predictions not available
from either component alone:

1. A computable coherence metric. For any boundary with known m and r, the dark
fraction δ(3m, r) is an exact rational number. No prior framework provides a quantitative
measure of how much of a system boundary’s configuration space is unmonitored.

2. Verification has computable diminishing returns. The marginal gain from the (r+1)-
th verification is (n choose r+1)/2ⁿ (Corollary 6). This tells an organization exactly how much additional
coverage each verification buys, and proves that after a threshold, further effort yields negligible
improvement.

3. Scale guarantees degradation. Corollary 5 proves that for any fixed verification bud-
get, adding variables to a boundary drives the dark fraction toward 1. Systems that grow in
complexity—more columns, more fields, more parameters—become less coherent even if verifi-
cation effort stays constant.

4. Inferred metadata does not reduce the dark fraction. A system that auto-tags a
column (e.g., inferring “transaction date” from a column named date) has not verified a facet.
It has produced a hypothesis inside the dark region. The dark fraction is unchanged. This is a
testable prediction: existing metadata tools that infer without confirming produce no reduction
in δ.

5 Future Work
The coherence unit admits typed specializations—a decision unit for decision intelligence, a
knowledge unit for knowledge graphs—each inheriting the same four-facet gauge. Because the
gauge is uniform across types, coherence units of different types can be composed and com-
pared using the same geometric framework. Exploring operations on networks of heterogeneous
coherence units is a natural next step.

6 Falsification Criteria
Criterion 1 (Completeness). Produce a boundary misalignment that does not manifest as
a URL mismatch on any of the three facets. This would show that n = 3m understates the
configuration space and δ is underestimated.

Criterion 2 (Isotropy). Produce a systematic case where operational cost depends on which
facets are misaligned, not just how many. This would show the Hamming ball is the wrong
shape and a weighted metric is needed. (The Hamming bound remains valid as a conservative
envelope.)

Criterion 3 (Compressibility). Build a working instrument that exploits correlations between
facets to compress the effective configuration space below 2³ᵐ. Until such an instrument exists,
the full cube is the space the organization operates in.

7 What This Does and Does Not Claim
Claims:
1. The four-facet protocol produces binary coordinates on the boundary configuration space.
2. The Hamming distance is the natural metric on this space—induced by the protocol, not
imposed.
3. The verified region is a Hamming ball with computable volume.
4. The dark fraction is computable and goes to 1 as variables grow.

Does not claim:
• That all misalignments are equally costly. (Payoff structure determines cost.)
• That the dark fraction equals risk. (It measures unverifiability, not expected loss.)
• That three facets are the only possible decomposition. (Alternative gauges yield different
cubes; the framework applies to any gauge.)
