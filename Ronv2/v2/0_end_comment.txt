Elegant name “Dark Fraction”, nice Hamming geometry, the corollaries about “scale guarantees degradation” and “diminishing returns” sound very scientific and “deep”.
Classic move: take a simple, useful idea (registering three types of metadata as URLs and comparing them), wrap it in a hypercube of Hamming distances and there you go – you have a “theorem”.
Someone might think that this flat decomposition is the “only correct” way to model context. In reality, it is a deliberate simplification that gives up hierarchy and dependencies in order to keep the computation easy.
Cost = number of comparisons performed = r
Risk = percentage of the configuration space you have no access to (dark fraction)
multiplied by $; that makes sense;
This is classic combinatorics from the binary cube – exactly the same as in error-correcting code theory (Hamming bound), just wrapped in a nice name “Dark Fraction” and put into a data context.
There is no deep semantics here, no complex ontology, no machine learning – just:
How many bits of information you managed to collect vs. how many bits of information exist in total at the boundary.
