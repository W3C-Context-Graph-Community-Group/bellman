The Dark Fraction Theorem:
A Geometric Bound on Unverifiable Configurations
at System Boundaries

April 2026
Working paper

Abstract:

We present a protocol that decomposes each variable at a system boundary into three binary facetsвҖ”Meaning, Structure, and ContextвҖ”and show that this decomposition induces a Hamming cube {0,1}Вібөҗ as the natural configuration space for m variables. Verification of r facets covers a Hamming ball whose volume is computable exactly. The ratio of ball to cubeвҖ”the verified fractionвҖ”goes to zero as m grows. The complementвҖ”the dark fractionвҖ”measures the portion of the configuration space that no within-system diagnostic can reach. This quantity is new: no prior framework provides a computable metric for unverifiable boundary configurations. The mathematics is standard. The contribution is the mapping from protocol to geometry and the predictions it enables.

Predictions

The mapping from four-facet protocol to Hamming geometry produces predictions not available from either component alone:

1. A computable coherence metric. For any boundary with known m and r, the dark fraction Оҙ(3m, r) is an exact rational number. No prior framework provides a quantitative measure of how much of a system boundary's configuration space is unmonitored.

2. Verification has computable diminishing returns. The marginal gain from the (r+1)-th verification is C(n,r+1)/2вҒҝ (Corollary ). This tells an organization exactly how much additional coverage each verification buys, and proves that after a threshold, further effort yields negligible improvement.

3. Scale guarantees degradation. Corollary  proves that for any fixed verification budget, adding variables to a boundary drives the dark fraction toward 1. Systems that grow in complexityвҖ”more columns, more fields, more parametersвҖ”become less coherent even if verification effort stays constant.

4. Inferred metadata does not reduce the dark fraction. A system that auto-tags a column (e.g., inferring "transaction date" from a column named date) has not verified a facet. It has produced a hypothesis inside the dark region. The dark fraction is unchanged. This is a testable prediction: existing metadata tools that infer without confirming produce no reduction in Оҙ.

Falsification Criteria

Criterion 1 (Completeness). Produce a boundary misalignment that does not manifest as a URL mismatch on any of the three facets. This would show that n = 3m understates the configuration space and Оҙ is underestimated.

Criterion 2 (Isotropy). Produce a systematic case where operational cost depends on which facets are misaligned, not just how many. This would show the Hamming ball is the wrong shape and a weighted metric is needed. (The Hamming bound remains valid as a conservative envelope.)

Criterion 3 (Compressibility). Build a working instrument that exploits correlations between facets to compress the effective configuration space below 2Вібөҗ. Until such an instrument exists, the full cube is the space the organization operates in.

What This Does and Does Not Claim

Claims:

-  The four-facet protocol produces binary coordinates on the boundary configuration space.
-  The Hamming distance is the natural metric on this spaceвҖ”induced by the protocol, not imposed.
-  The verified region is a Hamming ball with computable volume.
-  The dark fraction is computable and goes to 1 as variables grow.

Does not claim:

-  That all misalignments are equally costly. (Payoff structure determines cost.)
-  That the dark fraction equals risk. (It measures unverifiability, not expected loss.)
-  That three facets are the only possible decomposition. (Alternative gauges yield different cubes; the framework applies to any gauge.)
